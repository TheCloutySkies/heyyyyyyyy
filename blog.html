One hundred and thirty days away from home, away from the normal pattern. Away from the forces that shaped me. I never felt lost, I don’t know what I felt most of the time. Just different. There was a new system that was getting calibrated. New questions to ask, new air to breathe, new protons to process. I felt as though I was a fish getting thrown from my warm cozy tank, into a cool flowing river. I don’t quite feel the shock of the water, just the feeling of something new. Something new which now requires me to make it my master. I prepared six months for this very moment, the transfer from still, familiar comfort to rough, nomadic movement. To think that hundreds of springs and winters before my time, the natives of the very land I was about to traverse felt the inverse of my situation. Hunting and traveling, to a settled encampment. A place to sanctuary for the many people before me had experienced a curious transition, into a prison for the restless man. I needed to move. I could not sit still any longer. I was being called somewhere, no where specific really. Just not here.

 

But why?

 

I graduated from high school in 2022. Core 40 diploma, nothing special. I participated in track and field, a sprinter and high jumper. I lost some and I won some. While still in school I got my first real job, an order taker at Taco Bell. I continued to work there until about June or July of 2022. I originally applied to work there with a group of my friends. We all got the job and it was fun, a year and a half later and it was just me. I was working but not for a reason anymore, the novelty wore off and I felt stuck. I quit. I spent a month or two just running around with friends during the summer doing typical teenager stuff. I learned pretty quick that you spend money a lot faster when you aren’t making any back. So I had to begrudgingly search for another job.

Walmart. Honestly working at Walmart was great. I made friends pretty quick, the work was fun, and the pay was great for an 18 year old kid. The itch was still there, I knew I still felt stuck. I did a good job not acknowledging the feeling for a couple months until I had a brief, yet eye opening conversation with a coworker. It went something like this;

“Kevin, how long have you been working here?”
“Hmm, probably about 7 years now.”
“How long are you planning on working here for?”
“Honestly I don’t know. Probably for as long as they will let me stay.”
“Did you want to do something else before you started working here?”
“I probably did. I can’t remember now. Live moves too fast and Walmart is where your dreams go to die .”

At that moment I realized two things; I don’t want my dreams to die, and that I don’t even have a dream to begin with. I saw for the first time that I applied for these jobs just for the sake of getting a job. I only worked at Taco Bell because my friends were there and my dad was really getting on me about getting into the workforce. I wasn’t putting my money towards something, at least not anything meaningful to me. I needed to search. Now naturally the first thing my brain went to was traveling. I’ve done a decent amount of traveling in and out of the country to this point of my life. Out of country thanks to my mom, she was born and raised in the Philippines and would take occasional trips back to see family and would bring me and my brother along. Inside the country trips were thanks to my dad, he used to deliver trailers and RV’s around the U.S. (and sometimes Canada) with a big, loud, and blue Dodge 3500. Lots of rock music and surprise rumble strips whenever he caught me dozing off.

Well I don’t have a CDL, let alone a truck. The Philippines would be nice but it would only last a month max and I have no clue how to speak the language. Time to come up with alternatives. Hitchhiking? Could be dangerous, and there was always the possibility that I would just never get picked up. Train hopping? Even more dangerous, also could face jail time/fines. Road trip? Oh yeah i don’t have a car. Military? This was legitimately my second choice if I couldn’t figure something out. I would learn a lot of important skills and it would be a fun challenge too. The biggest deterrent for joining up is the water stuff, I am a BAD swimmer. I can manage a 50 yard survival, maximum struggle swim but beyond that and I am straight up dying. I really need to figure something out.

YouTube was founded in 2005 by three former PayPal employees as a platform to easily share and view videos online. In 2006, Google acquired YouTube, transforming it into the world’s largest video-sharing platform. Don’t we all just love YouTube? A habit I was beginning to form while at work was to have a YouTube video playing while I worked to help the time pass. I think one of my videos must’ve ended so I pulled out my phone and boom, my life changed. In the recommended videos grid sat an intriguing piece of media, “Two Years on a Bike 1/4, Martijn Doolaard.” I felt something click, the day was October 30th, 2022. I saw the future.

I just gotta reiterate, don’t we all just love YouTube? I had absolutely no idea where to even start when it came to this stuff. Hours upon hours of reading and watching tutorials finally brought me to a bike. The Poseidon Redwood. A fairly new bike that came up on the market. It was light, durable, had a sturdy and prepped frame for lots of mounting options, and most importantly it was budget friendly. I was able to get it on sale for $749. I really lucked out because most bikes in the bike touring spaces get real pricey, real fast. Now a fair warning, if you order this bike online like I did, you’ll have to assemble the bike yourself. I believe I had to attach the handlebars, front wheel, seat post and seat, pedals, and the chain. By the time you finish, you’ll already know your bike pretty well.

Now what about my stuff. How am I supposed to carry 75 pounds of gear with me? Bags. In a typical bike touring setup there are bags mounted all over a bike. The biggest bags on my bike were mounted on the sides of my rear rack called pannier bags. The word pannier actually comes from an old English word for bread basket. After a bunch of online deliberations, I eventually chose the Ortlieb Back Roller Classics. Pretty much the Lebron James of bike panniers. Actually a fun fact; I named my bike, Bichael Jordan. If Mike is short for Michael; then Bike must be short for Bichael. But that’s aside from the point here. I decided to also buy my handlebar bag from Ortlieb, mostly because I was going to keep my camera in it and I wanted to make absolutely sure it would not get wet. And let me tell you that both of my Ortlieb products did a mind blowing job of keeping my stuff dry. Those bags have been through 30 degree nights and 105 degree days, torrential downpours and weeks of sun, crashes, drops, hundreds of miles worth of dust and dirt, and they held up magnificently. For the rest of my gear I would say that they were, very water resistant, but when you are in the great plains with nowhere to hide from a whole days onslaught of rain. You start to see where you gears’ weak points are. Like with the Ibera Trunk Bag, I had a period of time in Rapid City, South Dakota where it no joke rained for nearly 3 days non stop. I made the mistake of leaving it out on a picnic table, naively assuming it was waterproof from previous experiences with shorter periods of precipitation. Long story short, I found out what a fish tank would look like if they had zippers. So if you gleam any lessons from this, if you can afford to invest in vetted—more expensive gear. Do it. All together if you were to buy the same stuff that I did, and brand new, the price would come out to be close to $5000. That being said though, about three quarters of this stuff I bought used online and I probably paid about half of that hypothetical $5000.

For a headlight, get a rechargeable light with a metal housing and it wouldn’t hurt to get multiple. I had two headlights on my setup, one mounted horizontally to the flatbar and another mounted vertically on drop bar that I used for extra coverage or as a flashing light depending on the conditions. For rear lights I think I had 4 different lights. One main flashing light that was mad bright. In addition to that I had a almost like accent lights pointed in different directions to make my bike more visible and easier to identify as a bike.

On the self defense side of safety, I recommend on having at least bear spray with you. When I had to wild/stealth camp I would always sleep with my fixed blade knife, flashlight, phone, and bear spray right next to my head. Luckily I never had to use any of my self defense tools on any animals or humans. I did once wake up to the sound of rustling on the side of my tent. I groggily picked my head up slightly to see if I could figure out what curious little critter was looking for food in my camp as I did make myself some beef stew earlier that day.

* Hmmmpf *

My fight or flight sense activated without warning. It took my still half asleep brain a second or two to figure out why it was suddenly ready throw hands.

 

* Hmmmpf , sniff sniff*
“Oh that’s a bear.” I thought to myself

The heavy breathing and powerful snuffling were unmistakable. In northern Montana, (Around Eureka) that meant one thing: grizzly bear. They can smell food from over 20 miles away. Maybe I shouldn’t have made that beef stew . I figured this was going to happen sooner or later so I weighed my options. I read somewhere that you should made a bunch of noise to startle the bear away. I also happened to read in a separate article that that was a great way to get mauled. Another article advised turning on your flashlight inside your tent to surprise and confuse the bear away. I also had a sneaky suspicion that course of action could cause the bear to become a little TOO curious. Turns out there’s a lot of ways to die to a bear, who would’ve thought. But eventually after careful and nervous consideration, I made up my mind. I looked at my watch and it read about 5am in the morning. I put my earbuds in and fell back asleep about 5 minutes later. I woke up a couple hours later with no sign of the bear anywhere. Now I’m not saying that ignoring the danger is the best course of action but I suppose it’s a lesson that preparedness doesn’t exclusively refer to the tools and gadgets. I had the proper gear but still decided to take a more passive route to decrease the chance of bleeding out in a nylon coffin. This story is also a testament to how comfy my sleep setup was. My Big Agnes sleeping pad is one of those big, inflatable ones. It packs down small, it’s so comfortable, and surprisingly rugged. My sleeping bag was actually sometimes a little too hot and did not pack down as well, it actually ended up taking half of a pannier bag worth of space. My favorite part of my setup though had to be the Nemo Fillo, a half inflatable/half foam pillow. I packs down incredibly well and you can adjust it for your preferred head position. I even used it to sleep on cold, hard concrete in the Hollywood streets. Heck I still use it in my bed now.

Now onto clothes and gadgets. Having the right clothes is super important for a long bike trip. For me, I started out simple: a couple of yellow t-shirts with the sleeves cut off as my riding shirts and one pair of padded bike shorts. They weren’t the tight, Tour De France kind—more rugged, with a tough exterior that held up the entire trip. They dried out pretty quickly too, which was a bonus.

Later, I switched out the t-shirts for some breathable tank tops made for more athletic purposes. I got them cheap at Walmart, and honestly, they worked great. You don’t need to drop a ton of money to stay comfortable on a bike. You’re going to be hot and sweaty no matter what, so you might as well wear stuff you don’t mind trashing. I also brought plenty of breathable, stretchy underwear and only packed Merino wool socks. Merino wool is awesome—it keeps your feet warm when it’s cold, wicks away sweat when it’s hot, and adds a nice bit of cushioning, especially if you end up doing some hiking after a long day of riding.

I had a couple of extra t-shirts for off days and hanging out in town. Weirdly, I only brought one pair of pants, but it worked out fine. For shoes, I relied on my Salomon boots. Fun fact: as I’m writing this segment in December 2024, I’m still wearing the same boots I started my bike trip in. They’ve got thousands (and I for real mean thousands) of miles on them, and somehow, they’re still going strong.

When it rained, I had a Marmot rain jacket that did the job. If you’re getting a rain jacket, make sure it has pit zips. Sweating inside a raincoat is so much worse than getting a little wet from the rain. I’d rather deal with rain than feel like I’m in a portable salt sauna. I also brought a puff jacket that could pack into itself, which was super convenient. I didn’t use it much since I was biking in the summer, but it was nice to have just in case.

For gadgets, one of my best finds was this small Anker power strip. It packs down tiny, and I used it to charge everything—lights, portable chargers, my phone, and even a Bluetooth speaker. Speaking of portable chargers, bring more than one. Trust me, having multiple is a lifesaver. I also tried using a portable solar panel strapped to my trunk bag. Honestly, it didn’t work as well as I hoped, even though it was in the sun all day. It was honestly pretty cheap so that’s probably on me.

When it came to laundry, I carried one of those travel-sized detergent bottles that you can find at Dollar Tree or really any grocery store. My trick? Every time I stayed at a Warm Showers house, I’d refill it. Same deal with shampoo and body wash—I just made a mix of shampoo, conditioner, and body wash in one bottle to save space. Another pro tip: double-check that the caps on your bottles are tight. Once, I hit a big bump, crashed, and later discovered that all my clothes were soaked in detergent. I had to wear the same clothes that I had on for a couple of days until I could wash everything. Lesson learned.

One random but clutch thing I brought: U.S. Army-issued dust goggles. I packed them for the desert in case of sandstorms (yeah kind of random but who knows), but they ended up being great for mountain passes. When you’re bombing down a hill at 30 to 35 mph, bugs are a real problem. The goggles saved me from getting smacked in the eyes, which, let’s be honest, is kind of important when you’re going that fast with nearly zero protection.

Oh, and here’s a solid water tip. If you’re using disposable water bottles, suck the air out, crush them down to take up less space. Then, before you hit a stretch where there’s no access to water for a while, just blow them back up and fill them up at a gas station. It’s an easy way to carry extra reserves for those long, dry gaps between towns.

May 14th 2023, I have a lot to learn. I feel like this first day was supposed to be overwhelming, but I got used to it once I got my hair cut off, about 12 miles away from home.

This is life now. The second I rounded the bend heading northbound from my childhood home, I had committed all the way. In my head it became impossible to turn around. The first day was moderately easy, 39 miles. The most I’ve ever ridden in one day up to that point. It was honestly crazy to me in that moment that I was able to pull it off. Day 2 was miserable , 53 miles. I only spent 7 hours on the road that day but it really did have me thinking twice. Was I really cut out for this? All the YouTube bike people said I’ll force myself into shape in no time. It sure didn’t feel like it. Thankfully I was greeted by a wonderful Super 8 motel, and was promptly told I had to be 21 to book a room (I was 19 at the time). Great. I turn around in pure defeat and head for the door. The front desk lady realizes I’m headed for a 110lb bike instead of a one ton car and graciously lends me a king sized suite. Nice .

By Day 3, I crossed into Illinois and my basic routine was set. Wake up, tear down/eat, ride for a few hours,take a break, ride, eventually find a spot to sleep. Most things get easier with time, finding a good place to sleep was an exception. When you have money, no problem. Out west in the barren plains of Wyoming, no problem. NFS (National Forest Service) and BLM (Bureau of Land Management) land, no problem. Effectively homeless on a one hundred ten pound bicycle with $700 that is supposed to last me potentially a year or two? Yeah maybe paying for a $60 hotel room like on my second day wasn’t going to cut it.

 

 

Slumber Blunder

Around Day 5-ish , I ran into my first slumber blunder. I finished up a wonderful evening exploring Buffalo Rock State Park. My phone glitched that afternoon, So I was temporarily stranded. I was helped by this dad and his two boys. A few YouTube tutorials later, my phone was fixed.

I rode my bike down this trail I found earlier that I had thought was nice and empty and started to set up camp. After my tent was up, I went to do a little sight seeing in this area that had “No People Past This Point” signs set up. About an hour later or so I hear a honking noise. I’m probably just imagining things. Honk . Nope, it’s close. I climbed over the hill and It’s the 2 kids from earlier. They explained that I can’t camp here and I was lucky I met them earlier. They pretty much just told me to slip on out before the real park people get here, So at around 10pm I find myself in an empty parking lot with no back up plan. My first real stressful situation of the trip, honestly I was kind of freaking out. I called all the homies, told them about my situation, really just to make myself feel less lonely. About 30 minutes later everyone needs to sleep, so now I’m alone again. I check Google Maps for places nearby, nothing. All I know right now is that I can’t sleep here. I gotta go somewhere else. I hop on the bike and start riding. At this point of the journey, the little stick cracks and scamper noises in the far away bushes still scare me. I decided randomly to open my voice memos app and start talking to fill up the empty space.

Guys, talking to yourself is not weird . It really helps me get out of my bubble of stress and take a look at my situation and how I am currently handling it. I stop in the cute little town of North Utica. There was one bar open but I was too anxious to walk in. I open Google Maps again and spot a truck stop with an RV park about a mile away from it. It’s risky but what other shot do I have? At midnight I’m sitting on the sidewalk in front of this gas station, I have a few little snacks and I have the website for the RV park open.

Long story short I was able to buy a reservation, slip into the park undetected and get an OK sleep. I went to the front office early in the morning and told them my current situation and I got a free day and another night to rest up. Just about every time you have to deal with a stressful situation, you will be rewarded as long as you work your way to the other side. There were so many times where I had similar situations to this one where the solution was to put my head down and cycle another 10-15 miles in the dark and stealth camp. Actually you know what, on that note here’s a quick guide to stealth camping.

One of the more influential people that I met on the road, I actually met only a few days into the journey. His name is Steven Beto , and he was walking from Minnesota all the way to Philadelphia and then eventually make his way to Boston, Massachusetts. What I didn’t know at the time is that a lot of the things that he was trying to tell me and educate me about, I actually started to hold very valuable in my current way of life. He talked about Sigmund Freud and Carl Jung, which are two people that I do find very influential on my current world view and I have actually now, at this point in writing, become quite interested in psychology, especially when it comes to analytical psychology, which Carl Jung actually started. It’s interesting looking back now, sometimes I wonder to myself if I somehow stumbled across a fortune teller of sorts. I thing I am sure of though, he was a darn good motivational speaker.

Example of What One Week of Riding Looks Like

I tried journaling for the first couple of weeks of my trip but quickly ran out of patience and energy. Here’s what the second week of my trip looked like.

 

 

Day 7: May 20th

 

32 miles

 

After the off day, I head back onto the I&M Canal Trail and use the laundromat in Peru, IL. After some more hilly cycling I make it to DePue, IL. I make a stop at the Casey’s and see this cool mural. More cycling ensues and I finally make it to the Hennepin Canal Trail and camp for the night at the Lock 6 campground

Day 8: May 21st

 

31 miles

 

First stop is Tiskilwa, a quick gas station food resupply and back on the Hennepin Canal Trail. 5 hours of pretty much nonstop cycling on the canal trail until I make it to Annawan. Luckily there is a gas station where I can sit down and charge my devices. I camp at the Hennepin Canal Bridge 23 site, which is only about a mile away from the gas station. Day 9: May 22nd

 

48 miles

 

Lots of cycling today. About 6 hours on the trail, making a pit stop in Geneseo. I reach my destination for the day at around 5pm , Fisherman’s Corner, right on the Mississippi.

Gnat intensity: 6.4/10.

40 miles

Around two and a half weeks in, it hits. Three or four social interactions a day ranging from a friendly nod to an intermediate length chat. A week earlier I met Mr. Beto. His only connection to the internet is library computers. His plans consist of; looking for the library the next town over, and then walk. That’s lonely.

He said all his family is either dead or moved away and the only person in his life he keeps in contact with (by email) is his son. I told him before I continued on that I would contact him and keep him updated, I suppose I should get to that soon. Loneliness is so fascinating. One moment it can be the most therapeutic experience. The next moment you are suffocating within the vast emptiness. The west is full of wide open spaces, jaw dropping beauty, all the stars in the galaxy swimming in the sky, wildlife, vivid sound, and a lot of the time, not a single human for hours. After a while you don’t associate cars and houses with people, they just kind of blend in with the surroundings. When you do finally come across someone there is kind of an internal struggle of being happy and grateful to interact with another person and just wanting to be left alone for just a little bit longer.

Music is a very critical tool that I use to stifle loneliness. I had a Bluetooth speaker that I attached to my handlebar bag with a lone bungee cord that quickly became a valuable asset. Music always helped me before the trip whenever I was working. It kind of gets me in a state where I can maintain a repetitive cadence for prolonged amounts of time. Very handy for 8+ hours a day of riding a bike. I looked into some studies regarding the matter, one conducted in Australia showed music listening was not only one of the most effective activities for improving individuals’ moods but also became a crucial tool for emotional regulation. Younger adults seemed to integrate music into their lives to express identity and navigate their emotions easier.

Additionally, a survey that took place on three continents highlighted how music served as a replacement for social interaction sometimes, especially for those with increased negative emotions. Music therapy is a form of therapy that uses music to address physical, emotional ,and social needs of individuals. Trained music therapists use a variety of musical interventions, such as listening to music to enhance the overall well-being of clients. It is applied in all sorts of fields with the aim of improving mood, causing relaxation, and supporting emotional expression and healing. Music therapy sessions were found to provide a unique form of communication, offering a sense of control, creativity, and comfort.

I know a lot of us first hand can definitely attest to the effects of music, especially when you are laying in bed late at night. The effects really get cranked up when you’re utterly alone for weeks on end. I would go whole days where there had to be some sort of audio playing because I needed something to fill the air around me. Inversely though, sometimes pure silence can have the same calming effect as your favorite song or comfort podcast.

The crushing effects of loneliness really hit me hardest in the vast wilderness of Montana. I developed this very negative head space while rolling through Wyoming and Montana. I was put into so many extremely tough and taxing situations that truly tested my mental fortitude. Money was just beginning to become an issue, my body was aching trying to cover as much ground as possible. Not to say I didn’t have my moments of bliss though, seeing gorgeous mountains, real mountains, for the first time in my life and all by my own power. I’d met amazing people and had some interesting interactions, but a pit was opening up. All of these issues and stressors were adding up, even the small inconveniences were heavy.

These small towns became huge cities, almost cold and uninviting. Shunning me out into the wild again once I shilled over my dwindling supply of currency. Every friendly, food bearing, mattress providing, air conditioned Warmshowers host acted as a reminder of the comfort I had abandoned. The mattresses, pillows, and blankets just delighted to see me on the road again, as to serve as a lesson to never forsake their embrace again. It was rough and to be honest, I cried a few times. Even when the most beautiful mountains I’ve ever laid my eyes upon rested ahead of me, I was sometimes hard to even push out a smile. For example at Glacier National Park. I honestly felt like I had lost my way. Not in a physical sense but in a purpose kind of way. I probably spent most of that day complaining, about what? Who cares. I had finally slipped past the gates in the cover of darkness and was descending upon my campsite. I felt the tickle of tears in my head, I was so over it, I had to vent. I powered on my phone, opened voice memos, and tapped on that familiar red circle.

I think I found my campsite—site 129. But yeah, I just don’t like how I’m feeling right now. I’ve realized something about life: most of the things we do to validate ourselves are done in vain. There aren’t many things that can truly satisfy a person.

At the same time, I’m grateful for this opportunity to explore myself. This journey has hardened me physically and mentally, but it’s also opened me up to new ideas and made me better at connecting with people. I’ve seen how generous, forgiving, and beautiful people can be.

In America, everything revolves around money, and it feels like I barely have enough to get by. Today, for example, I snuck into Glacier. There’s a bike trail that leads right to this campground I reserved. It’s like I’m just riding this strange streak of luck.

And about luck… I’ve been thinking about it a lot. I don’t think it’s just blind luck anymore. I’ve been so fortunate, consistently, that it doesn’t make sense. It feels like something is guiding me—some force that’s influencing these events. It’s made me more spiritual, more aware. Maybe it’s God giving me little nudges. I don’t know. But I shouldn’t even be here; I shouldn’t have made it this far. Yet here I am, in Glacier National Park.

I’m constantly struggling with money, though. I barely have enough for food. I’ll go days where I’m painfully hungry, to the point that I just ride my bike until my legs hurt enough to distract me. Then I try to fall asleep and deal with it tomorrow. Recently, I spent $13 at Walmart on granola bars, protein bars, and some drink mix. It was cheap and it’ll last me a few days, but it’s not enough.

On my worst days, I spend money in more than one place. On my best days, I spend no money at all. Usually, I eat one meal a day—sometimes none—and snack through the pain until I can sleep it off. I can eat a whole pizza in 20 minutes and still feel hungry immediately after. That’s when I realized how malnourished I am.

It’s a vicious cycle: the more frugal I am, the more pain I endure. If I spend more money, the pain temporarily goes away. I guess I’ve programmed myself to believe that’s the trade off. And it’s not just physical pain—it’s mental and emotional too. I’ve put myself through so much strain to get this far. Some days it feels worth it; other days, it doesn’t.

Friends have told me they want to walk to Yellowstone, almost a thousand miles. A part of me wants to encourage them, but another part—especially how I’m feeling now—wants to tell them not to do it. You don’t need some grand adventure to fix yourself. There are other ways to find satisfaction and peace.

Today is Day 69 of this journey. I can’t believe I’ve been doing this for so long with practically no plan—just winging it. But everything costs money, and money is something I don’t have. Money solves a lot of problems in America, especially hunger and bike repairs.

Water, at least, is easier to find. Americans generally believe everyone has a right to water, even if they don’t believe in free access to food. And access to nature? Forget it. I’ve already complained enough about that.

I’ve been thinking about ending this trip. I’m not feeling the same as I used to. Glacier will be beautiful—I know it will—but I feel like I can always come back later when I’m better prepared, when I won’t risk being penalized for being broke.

Most, no all of the bad things that have happened to me are because of my own decisions. The good things? I attribute most of them to God. Maybe that turns you off, but I’m not preaching. I’m just telling you what I’ve seen. For every bad thing, there’s been ten great things. I’ve started keeping a gratitude journal, and it’s easy to do. We take so much for granted.

I hope, even if you don’t believe in God, you’re still listening. Maybe next time you think, “Wow, that’s lucky,” take a second to look at it differently. Look around. Think about it again. It can’t just be luck. There have been too many little things—tiny, perfect moments—that align too well to be a coincidence.

I’m just grateful. That’s all I can say. Thank you.”

Now, I suppose to preface, loitering is frowned upon, and sometimes it’s illegal. By loitering, the definition I would give is; using a given location’s resources for the longest acceptable time. Those resources could include electricity, food, water, WiFi, a place to sit, bathrooms, laundry, heat/air conditioning, and so on. If there is a No Loitering sign posted, that just means try and look like you have a reason to be there . Don’t just sit and do absolutely nothing in front of a business. That’s how you get your precious rest stop abruptly ended. Here is my list on places you should typically be fine for loitering a short-ish amount of time. Travel centers/rest stops (Flying J/Pilot, Buc-ee’s, TA, etc.) They’re literally meant for people to spend hours at just to hang around. Some smaller gas stations also have places to sit), fast food restaurants, coffee shops, city parks, libraries, Walmart super centers, some churches, lodges/resorts, and big campgrounds where you could typically find rich people in their RV’s. Now, out of that list, the top three places I would consider loitering would probably probably be gas stations , public parks , and libraries . Here are a few of my favorite parts about each of those options.

 

Gas stations . Gas stations provide electricity, seating, food and water, bathrooms, sometimes showers and laundry services, Wi-Fi , air conditioning and heat. The gas stations that you typically want to look for should be pretty large, and preferably have a fresh food option. A $2 slice of pizza, and a $1.29 bottle of chocolate milk can go a long way. Also, so look into the freezer section for cheap Hot Pockets and burritos. Lots of calories for not a lot of money, and a lot of gas stations typically have microwaves. You can usually find some cheap ramen which I’ve noticed always seems to be suspiciously close to the cans of dog food. In the morning, get a cheap coffee, they’re usually not any more than a dollar or two unless you get a really large size. Practically every gas station I went to would let me fill my water bottles for free, either in some sort of sink or even from the drink machine. What I would normally do is pretty much fill my bottles entirely up with ice and water then put a little squirt of pop in there to keep it sweet. If you’re the gambling type, you could also always buy some scratch off tickets to test your luck. If you do end up around a larger gas station especially closer to the interstate, look around for brochures and maps. You can usually find them around the register or near the restrooms.

 

Parks . Public parks typically will provide electricity, a place to sit, water, basic, shelter interesting people, and sometimes bathrooms, and brochures a place to make a little fire. Now, public parks don’t offer as much as gas stations, but at least they offer a little peace and quiet. At the very least, the cool parks will have electricity at pavilions or the bathrooms. The bathrooms are typically arranged from being okay to a literal war zone. A lot of the time you can take a nap for a while, but be sure to sleep lightly because depending on the area that you’re in, you can get your stuff yoinked. You can typically find water in the bathrooms, sometimes on the walls of the bathrooms from a spigot or a dedicated water fountain. If you happen to be at a sports complex like a soccer or a baseball field, there’s a chance you could find a pop machine and bigger water spigots.

 

Libraries . Libraries provide shelter, security, water, bathrooms, WiFi, heating/air conditioning, and a comfortable place to sit. I always love a good library. Probably the friendliest place you can go, and it’s definitely the quietest. Plenty of places to sit and charge everything. Access to computers as well. If you’re a bike traveler like I was, there’s almost always a place out front to park your bike at a bike rack. The bathrooms are always so nice and typically have a water fountain around nearby as well. Some libraries will have an area with free books and sometimes pamphlets for the local areas, so you might be able to snag yourself a map and at the very least, some stuff to make fires with. Typically the librarians are pretty knowledgeable of the area, being locals of whatever town or city you’re in, so they can be a valuable resource when it comes to finding places to eat or set up camp for the night.

The road is dangerous.

Cars, animals, debris, unexpected weather, road closages , wild fires, poisonous plants, violent individuals, dehydration, cacti, falling rocks, broken spokes, flat tires, failing brakes, 11% grades, low visibility, pot holes, money problems, no phone service, people stealing your Sony XB-33 Bluetooth speaker in Florence, Oregon, and getting lost. All things I had to contend with on my journey. How am I supposed to move forward knowing that only more dangers lie ahead . How do I know there’s not homeless people at the next place I want to camp? I dont . I suppose when it comes down to bike travel and wild camping, there’s no way you are entirely avoiding cars and animals and things of that sort.

I think a good place to start is to identify the least comfortable position that you are willing to tolerate for a night . For me one example was walking around Hollywood all night long, blending in with the homeless population, until the morning came. I did that for two nights and for one of those nights it rained. For the three total nights I spent in Hollywood, I aimlessly walked around for about 36 miles.

Now this next point may seem vague and kind of obvious. ALWAYS practice situational awareness at all times . Take mental notes of all the sights, sounds and smells of where you are. Give your attention to things that break the normal patterns. Areas where you can take your tent, excessive trash, signs of frequent foot traffic, road signs, broken glass, animal/human poop. Anything that could indicate potential danger should be noted .

This last tip cannot be easily explained or taught, so I will just leave you with this. Listen to your instincts . If something feels off, proceed with caution. If you are in the wild and your hair and ears prick up, stop and observe your surroundings for a moment. I don’t know how many instances where listening to that little gut feeling has gotten me out of potentially bad situations.

  • Move when it feels right.
  • Stay put if it feels right.
  • If you feel as if you are in danger, you probably are .
  • Only get comfortable if you feel comfortable.

I really think our gut feelings are just our subconscious. It’s trying its best to communicate with us and I advise we listen. I’ve heard theorized that when we get that feeling that we are being watched, it’s because we noticed something watching us but our brain tries to focus on the “most important” tasks that are directly effecting us. So yeah think about that the next time you’re out in the woods and you feel those pits open up in the back your head.
